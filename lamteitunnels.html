Lam Tei Tunnels
2014

“As soon as the T.A.Z. is named (represented, mediated), it must vanish, it will vanish, leaving behind it an empty husk, only to spring up again somewhere else, once again invisible because undefinable in terms of the Spectacle. The T.A.Z. is thus a perfect tactic for an era in which the State is omnipresent and all-powerful and yet simultaneously riddled with cracks and vacancies.” — Hakim Bey, T.A.Z

The project explores tunneling as both a form of resistance and a form of control. Taking Peter Lamborn Wilson’s T.A.Z: Temporary Autonomous Zone and something he calls “the Will to Power as Disappearance” as inspiration, the project attempts to visualize the power struggle between the hierarchical power of the state and the ad hoc, decentralized, rhizomatic logic of alternative societies in tunnels within the Hong Kong mountains. While in conflict, these two forms of organization are not a dialectic. They exist as a mixture. Each type is continually being converted into the other.

In order to exist off the grid in 2047, societies will tunnel into the Hong Kong mountains to take advantage of the shielding qualities of the granite. Those who participate desire to escape electromagnetic radiation, surveillance, or the spectacle as a whole. Disappearance is their source of power: “the most radical tactic will be not to engage in spectacular violence but to withdraw from the area of simulation, to disappear” (Wilson). Eventually, what began as a technique to escape the spectacle is co-opted by the financial system to remain free after Hong Kong loses its status as a Special Administrative Region in 2047.

Quarrying is a fraught process in Hong Kong because of the positive Feng Shui associated with the mountains. Lam Tei Quarry in the New Territories is currently the only operating quarry and is scheduled to close in the next ten years. This scar on the mountain becomes the entry point for the tunnels of the state.
