Harlem Fields
2013

The project proposes an assembly of low-rise, high density slabs that respond to and reshape their urban context. It re-imagines the edge of the city as a field of interaction, repetition and communalism that potentially leads to new urbanisms, communities and possibilities.

The project offers an alternative to the figure-ground relationship of the tower in the park by breaking apart the tower and bringing the park up into the tower in a landform-type operation. The goal is to create a relatively low-rise but dense field of interactions where figure and ground and mass and void oscillate. Objects become the ground for other objects while the ground itself infiltrates the form of the building. In Log 27, Peter Trummer writes:

“In the city as an aggregated object, every building provides the access, the construction and the ground for the next building. Like every room in Alberti’s house, every building defines the condition of the next. In such a city we cannot distinguish between ground and figure, common and individual space, solid and void. When the architectural object becomes both figure and ground, the city becomes and aggregated object, an object unified by the buildings performing as grounds and figures.” 1

This approach provides the framework for this urban project.

Because the site conditions of the elevated train and the highway preclude the continuous street wall of the typical NYC block, the project attempts to offer an alternative to the linear townhouse and the perimeter block. The project attempts to densify and urbanize the linear by using walls to create a field. It penetrates the urban block to offer a variety of spacings, connections and groupings. It brings what is most commonly associated with the ground floor of a building up into the project. Communal spaces are broken off the units to facilitate interaction and an impetus towards sharing. The units are marked by efficiency but are also connected to communal spaces for different types of meetings and amenities. The Nolli map is used as a tool to create an alternative to the typical public-private binary of an urban building.

The units feature a binary sidedness of load-bearing wall with punched windows on one side versus columns and curtain wall on the other side. This structural binary serves to control internal communities and clusters while unifying the form of what began as an aggregation of individual units.

1Trummer, Peter. “The City as an Object: Thoughts on the Form of the City.” Log 27 (New York: Anyone Corporation, 2013) 57.
